I.   Shape Reconstruction

Shapes

Pretty much whatever we see can be called a shape. In topology, shapes are distinguished by the topological features they manifest. The best class of shapes one always hopes to deal with is smooth manifolds smoothly embedded in Euclidean space.
For example, the smooth closed curve embedded in the plane on the left and the hollow torus embedded in the space on the right.

Smooth Manifolds (the good ones)
However, more often than not, practical applications deal with shapes that are far from being a smooth manifold. The map of the city of New Orleans can be modelled as a graph, where the intersections are thought of as the vertices, whereas the roads the edges.
Clearly, a graph is NOT a manifold -- because of the branchings at the intersections and the corners.
As our story unfolds, we will see that our reconstruction results can handle graphs -- as well as more sophisticated and general class of shapes -- which we call geodesic spaces.

Our shapes of interest (the ugly ones)

Geodesic Spaces

So, we first get the definition straight.
In geometry, a geodesic mimics the concept of SHORTEST PATH between two points on a graph.
Explain the picture.
It is evident from the definition that a graph is a geodesic space, so is any compact, sub-manifold of the Euclidean space. Higher dimensional simplicial complexes are also examples of geodesic spaces.
For the curious audience, the sphere -- if punctured at a point -- no longer remains a geodesic space.
For any two points $x,y\in X\subseteq\mathbb{R}^N$, there exists a geodesic or shortest path.
With the definition of the geodesic spaces under our belts, it's time to quickly squirrel away two very important properties -- or rather attributes that become indispensible in the theory ahead.
Define ...
To give you the perspective, the convexity radius of a circle of unit length is a quarter. Similarly, the convexity radius of a graph is ...
For a geodesic space $X\subseteq\mathbb{R}^N$, the distortion $$\delta=\sup_{x\neq y}\frac{d_L(x,y)}{\|x-y\|}.$$
For a geodesic space $X\subseteq\mathbb{R}^N$, the convexity radius $\rho$ is the half of the (ant) distance between a pair of furthest points $x,y\in X$ that have a unique geodesic.

Problem Statement

Here is how we formulate the problem of Euclidean shape reconstruction from a point-cloud.
  • Let $X$ be a hidden subset of $\mathbb{R}^N$
  • We have a sample or a point-cloud $(S,\|\cdot\|)$
  • $S$ is dense around $X$.
Q: Can we infer the toplogy of $X$ from $S$? What does inference even mean in such a reconstruction context?
  • Compute $H_*(X)$ and/or $\pi_*(X,b)$ from $S$
  • Construct $\widetilde{X}$ homotopy equivalent to $X$
  • $\widetilde{X}$ has a geometrically-close embedding in $\mathbb{R}^N$

Density via Hausdorff Distance

Before I show you the reconstruction results, it's time to enforce some density condition on the sample. We can ask for a small Hausdorff distance between the sample and the unknown shape of interest.
The concept of the Hausdorff distance was first introduced by --- I guess --- Hausdorff himself.
Hausdorff distance $d_H^\mathbb{R^2}(A,B)$ is
$\max\{$ $a$, $b$ $\}=$ $b$
Let $A,B\subseteq(Z,d_Z)$. Their Hausdorff distance is defined by
$d_H^Z(A,B)=\max\{$ $\sup_{a\in A}\inf_{b\in B}d_Z(a,b)$, $\sup_{b\in B}\inf_{a\in A}d_Z(a,b)$$\}.$

If $d_H^Z(A,B) <\varepsilon$, then
each point $a\in A$ has a point $b\in B$ with $d_Z(a,b) <\varepsilon$, and
each point $b\in B$ has a point $a\in A$ with $d_Z(a,b) <\varepsilon$.

Vietoris-Rips Complex

For a metric space $(M,d_M)$ and scale $\varepsilon>0$, each subset $A$ of size $k$ with $diam_d(A)\leq\epsilon$ is a $(k-1)$-simplex of the Vietoris-Rips complex $R_\varepsilon(M)$.
Let $X$ be a smooth sub-manifold. If $\epsilon>0$ is small and $d_H(X,S)$ is also small, then the Vietoris-Rips complex $R_\varepsilon(S)$ is homotopy equivalent to $X$.

Our Approach

  • $X\subseteq\mathbb{R}^N$ is a geodesic subspace $(X,d_L)$
  • Distortion $\delta$ is finite
  • Convexity radius $\rho$ is positive
  • $d_H(X,S)$ is small

Topological Reconstruction

Instead of looking at Rips complex only at one scale epsilon, we take two scales. We first collect the cycles at the lower scale. There might be some anomalous cycles --- but we kill them at the larger scale. At the end, the surviving cycles generate the desired homology groups of the unknown geodesic space X.
Such an idea of accounting for two scales is known as Persistent Homology. However, the novelty here is to recognize the two sampling parameters.
If $d_H(X,S) <\epsilon<\frac{\rho}{2\delta(3\delta+1)}$, the image $$Im(j_*)\simeq\pi_*(X),$$ where $j:\mathcal{R}_{\epsilon}(S)\to\mathcal{R}_{\frac{1}{2}(3\delta+1)\epsilon}(S)$.

Geometric Reconstruction

If $d_H(G,S) <\epsilon<\frac{\rho}{2\delta(3\delta+1)}$, we can output $\widetilde{X}$ such that $$\widetilde{X}\simeq G,$$ and $d_H(G,\widetilde{X})$ is small.

Gromov-Hausdorff Distance


How to compare shapes that do not have a common embedding?

Isometric Embedding

For two abstract metric spaces $(X,d_X)$ and $(Y,d_Y)$, we define $$d_{GH}(X,Y)=\inf\limits_{f,g,Z} d^Z_H(f(X),g(Y)).$$

$d_{GH}$ vs $d_{H,iso}$

Let $X,Y\subseteq\mathbb{R}^N$ be compact. Is $d_{GH}(X,Y)=d_{H,iso}(X,Y)$?

ðŸ’¡

  • memoli_theoretical_2005: $d_{GH}(X,Y)\neq d_{H,iso}(X,Y)$ for $N=2,3,\ldots$.
  • What happens if $N=1$?
For compact $X,Y\subseteq\mathbb{R}^1$, we have $$d_{GH}(X,Y)\leq d_{H,iso}(X,Y)\leq\left(1+\frac{1}{4}\right)d_{GH}(X,Y),$$ and the upper bound is tight.

Computing $d_{GH}$

For $|X|,|Y|\leq k$, computing $d_{GH}(X,Y)$ takes $O(2^k)$ time.
For $X,Y\subset\mathbb{R}^1$ and $|X|,|Y|\leq k$, computing $d_{H,iso}(X,Y)$ takes $O(k\log{k})$ time.
ðŸ’¡

$d_{H,iso}$ approximates $d_{GH}$ with an approximate factor of $\left(1+\frac{1}{4}\right)$.
